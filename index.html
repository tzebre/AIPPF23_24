Started in the early 60’s in secret by Woodrow “Woody” Bledsoe in the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA), research about facial recognition has quickly evolved and found important usage in our daily lives.

In the early daysfacial recognition didn’t even use computing methods, it was only an assembly of manual measurements between different facial points. This identification technique evolved in parallel with Artificial intelligence and deep learning improvement, leading to quicker and better identification relying on more features.

Today, thanks to improved hardware, facial recognition can be used on a wide range of hardware such as computers, drones, or cameras. It uses a wide range of technologies such as Haar or thermal images to extract features. However, this emerging technology is increasingly raising ethical and security issues.

Facial recognition in airport-Thales

Thales, one of France's largest companies, is working on the development of facial recognition for use in airports. They would use smart gates to identify people via their faces, enabling users to use their biometric passports for identity checks. This would enable faster checks without any action on the part of the user, while at the same time providing a higher level of reliability thanks to advanced recognition algorithms. However, this will not replace all human actions overnight but will complement them to facilitate the role of customs officers. The live photo taken during the identity check is stored on a server and then compared with the subject before he or she boards the plane. But there are problems associated with this system. For example, we don't know how long a photo is stored on a server, and if a person doesn't want their photo stored on the company's servers, how do they board the plane? What's more, if the storage server leaks, then an ill-intentioned person will be able to obtain all the information linked to a person's identity, as well as what he or she looks like. What's more, after a certain age, passports are kept for longer and longer by individuals without needing to be renewed, so the algorithm needs to be able to make a link between a more recent 2D photo of the person and their current 3D face, which will be transformed by age. The algorithm must be able to identify these two people as being the same. This is also a concern for people undergoing cosmetic surgery, as the results of the algorithm's calculations may differ. The algorithm must be based on variables that cannot be changed by the subject, otherwise it becomes obsolete.

Face ID-Apple

Apple has made a big step in implementing facial recognition in our daily use with Face ID. This cutting-edge technology is an example of a hardware and software combination to improve facial recognition performance leading to one error out of one million. A combination of different captors such as an IR camera, helps to create a 3D map of the face and a precise 2D mapping. On the ethics and security aspect, apple ensure that all the biometrics data are only stocked on the phone, also Apple has implemented an attention awareness feature that implies the user to investigate the phone to unlock it and thus reduce the risk of forced unlocking by a tier.

Mass monitoring-law enforcement authorities

The extent of possible surveillance by law enforcement authorities has significantly increased with the advent of CCTV and facial recognition technologies, as well as machine learning algorithms. This is very beneficial to society since it encourages the maintenance of security by making it simple to find those who are committing crimes. However, it also raises more and more worries about how it may affect our personal information, privacy, and communities—especially communities of color. From the federal to the municipal levels, numerous government organizations rely on tools created by the private sector to access massive volumes of data from social media, smartphones, and private cloud servers. There are verifiable instances of commercial businesses offering facial recognition and data collecting services to government authorities in the USA case, including Clearview AI, Vigilant Solutions, and ODIN Intelligence. Due to ethical concerns, a number of significant tech companies, including Amazon, Microsoft, and IBM, have pledged to either temporarily or permanently stop selling face recognition technology to law enforcement. This, however, calls into doubt the efficacy of such efforts, since other businesses might continue to provide these technologies to government organizations. In addition, we must consider the ethical and privacy consequences, raising issues with database bias, technological accuracy, and the fundamental question of whether to employ face recognition technology at all, even as accuracy increases.

Recognitions systems on drone-US Army

The US Army has partnered with a company called RealNetworks to create autonomous drones equipped with facial recognition systems. The aim of this approach is benevolent, as it can help enormously in intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance missions. A drone can be sent out to identify criminals in hostile areas and theaters of war, without having to risk human life every time. Technologically speaking, this implies having to deploy and design a software and hardware stack capable of fitting on a single drone, including its on-board computer, communication systems and remote-control software to enable operation in communication environments, allow remote human operators to obtain exploitable information and open the possibility of real-time autonomous response on the part of the robot. However, this leads us to a very big negative point. The contract between the army and the company talks of equipping unarmed drones for the time being, but once the technology has been developed, there's nothing to stop them developing it on armed drones capable of killing. This raises a very big ethical question: should the drone be allowed to make the decision to kill its target if identification is positive? Depending on the country and the different laws, the drone could take this decision to take life on its own, while in other countries, human confirmation would be required before taking any action. The problem here is not directly linked to facial recognition, but rather to all the drifts that could result from it, both good and bad.

Upon initial observation, there are a huge number of facial recognition algorithms and methods. They are all more or less complex, have different learning methods and retain data for varying lengths of time. As part of this project for the GE2340 course, we chose to show how to implement a facial recognition algorithm on your computer. For our approach, we selected the Viola-Jones Face Detection Technique, also known as Haar Cascade, for two reasons: first, it's an extremely efficient algorithm and, second, it fully meets the ethical concerns we had in mind.
